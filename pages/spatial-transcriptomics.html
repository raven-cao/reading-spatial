Spatial Transcriptomics

Gene expression, with location preserved

The problem it solves

Standard scRNA-seq dissociates tissue into a single-cell suspension — spatial context is destroyed. You learn what cell types exist and in what proportions, but not where they are relative to each other. A T cell that was sitting 20µm from a tumor cell looks identical in the data to one that was 500µm away, even if their behavior is completely different.

Spatial transcriptomics restores the city map. Named Nature Methods Technology of the Year in 2022.

How it works (in-situ imaging approach)

Tissue section is placed on a slide. Fluorescent probes — one designed for each target gene — hybridize to their complementary mRNAs directly in the tissue without dissociation. Each probe carries a color barcode. A fluorescence microscope scans the section, recording signal locations. Combined with DAPI nuclear staining to identify cell boundaries, each mRNA signal is attributed to a specific cell. Output: a table where each row is a cell, columns are gene counts, plus (x,y) coordinates.

What the data looks like

Each cell = (x, y) coordinates + 6,000-dimensional expression vector. The coordinates tell you where it lives in the tissue; the vector tells you what it's currently doing. Both are required for AMICI.

Why this matters for T cell biology
A T cell in the tumor core and a T cell at the tumor margin may both be labeled "CD8+ T cell" in scRNA-seq data. In spatial data, you discover they have completely different expression profiles — core cells exhausted, margin cells effector. The position is not just metadata; it explains the biology.

Spatial vs scRNA-seq

They're complementary, not competing. scRNA-seq gives deeper sequencing (more genes per cell, more cells per dollar). Spatial transcriptomics gives position but typically shallower coverage. The CosMx 6k panel is a targeted panel — 6,000 pre-selected genes, not the full ~20,000 transcriptome.

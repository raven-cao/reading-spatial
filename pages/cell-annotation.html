Technology & Methods

Cell Annotation

Assigning biological identity to clusters of cells

The pipeline

Step 1: Dimensionality reduction (UMAP). A 6,000-dimensional vector per cell is impossible to plot. UMAP compresses to 2D while preserving relative distances between cells. Similar cells appear close together.

Step 2: Clustering (Leiden algorithm). Automatically identifies groups of cells that cluster together — "clumps" in the UMAP space.

Step 3: Find marker genes per cluster. For each cluster, find genes significantly more expressed there than in all others. Cluster 3 might show CD8A, CD3E, GZMB at the top.

Step 4: Human annotation. A researcher cross-references marker genes with databases (CellMarker, PanglaoDB) and literature to name each cluster. CD8A + CD3E → CD8+ T cell. EPCAM + KRT20 → epithelial/tumor cell. This step requires biological expertise and is inherently interpretive.

Why marker genes work

Cell type identity is determined by which genes a cell needs to function. T cells must express CD8A and TCR components to do their job — recognizing and killing targets. Epithelial cells must express EPCAM for cell-cell adhesion. These functional requirements produce stable, reproducible expression signatures. Marker genes aren't arbitrary labels; they're the functional machinery of the cell type.

Where uncertainty enters
The same tissue analyzed with different clustering parameters can yield different numbers of clusters with different boundaries. Cells in transitional states may share markers from two types. The final annotation is the researcher's best inference, not ground truth — and this uncertainty propagates to every downstream analysis including AMICI.

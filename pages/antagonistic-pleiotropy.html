Cancer Biology

Antagonistic Pleiotropy

Why evolution can't fix cancer — and the trade-off written into our genes

The concept

Pleiotropy: one gene, multiple effects. Antagonistic: those effects oppose each other — beneficial early in life, harmful later. Natural selection "sees" reproductive success, not longevity. A gene that boosts fertility at age 25 but increases cancer risk at 60 will be selected for — selection pressure disappears after reproduction.

Why cancer risk accumulates with age

Most solid tumors peak in incidence at 50–70 years. Evolutionary selection pressure essentially ends at ~35 (post-reproductive in ancestral environments). Mutations that cause cancer at 60 are invisible to selection. They persist in the gene pool because they never encounter a fitness cost.

A concrete example

IGF-1 signaling promotes cell growth, tissue repair, and reproductive development in young organisms — strongly selected for. In older organisms, the same "promote cell growth" signal drives tumor progression. The gene doesn't distinguish. Evolution optimized the first part and couldn't fix the second.

My framing
Evolution made a deal: great early-life performance in exchange for late-life deterioration. The deal is encoded in the genome and can't be unilaterally renegotiated by the individual. This reframes aging and cancer not as failures but as consequences of a selection process that had no incentive to look beyond peak reproductive age.

MSS vs MSI

Why one subtype responds to immunotherapy and the other doesn't

Microsatellites and mismatch repair

Microsatellites are short tandem repeat sequences in the genome (e.g., CACACACA...). When DNA replicates, the polymerase sometimes slips on these repeats, adding or deleting a repeat unit. The mismatch repair (MMR) system normally detects and corrects this. If MMR is defective (mutation or epigenetic silencing of MMR genes like MLH1, MSH2), errors accumulate across the genome. Microsatellites are especially vulnerable — they expand or contract in length. This genome-wide instability is called microsatellite instability (MSI).

Why MSI means more mutations

MMR deficiency doesn't just affect microsatellites — it affects the whole genome. Protein-coding genes accumulate frameshift mutations, many of which create novel peptide sequences (neoantigens) that the immune system can recognize as non-self.

The immune response difference

MSI: High neoantigen load → immune system has many targets → robust T cell infiltration → some effector T cells persist despite suppressive signals → responds to checkpoint inhibitors (which amplify the existing response).

MSS: Low mutation burden → fewer neoantigens → less immune activation → T cells that do infiltrate face overwhelming suppression → don't respond to checkpoint inhibitors (there's no latent response to amplify).

The question this project asks
Is the spatial organization of the tumor-immune interface different in MSI vs. MSS? Specifically — which cell-cell interactions in MSI tumors help T cells maintain effector state, and which interactions in MSS tumors drive exhaustion? AMICI on CosMx data can map these interactions at single-cell resolution.

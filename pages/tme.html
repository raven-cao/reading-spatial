Immune System

Tumor Microenvironment

The ecosystem that shapes immune function — and immune failure

What it is

A tumor is not a mass of cancer cells in a void. It's a local ecosystem containing: cancer cells, CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, regulatory T cells, macrophages, dendritic cells, cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs), blood vessels, and the extracellular matrix. Each component influences the others.

Why the tumor core is immunosuppressive

Dense antigen exposure. Tumor cells constantly display neoantigens. T cells are perpetually stimulated — no "mission complete" signal arrives. Chronic stimulation drives exhaustion.

Hypoxia. Tumors grow faster than their blood supply. Low oxygen activates HIF-1α, which upregulates PD-L1 expression on tumor cells — hypoxia directly amplifies the immunosuppressive signal.

PD-L1 signaling. Tumor cells express PD-L1, which binds PD-1 on T cells. This is a hijacked self-tolerance mechanism — normally protects healthy tissue from autoimmune attack. Tumors evolved to exploit it.

Result: T cells in the core enter the exhaustion program — TOX activated, PD-1/TIM-3/CTLA4 upregulated, GZMB/IFNG downregulated. The immune system is present but disarmed.

Tumor margin vs. tumor core

The margin (interface between tumor and healthy tissue) has different signals: lower antigen density, access to dendritic cell co-stimulation, less severe hypoxia. T cells there may still be in effector state. This spatial heterogeneity — invisible to scRNA-seq — is one reason spatial transcriptomics changes the questions we can ask.

Connection to AMICI
AMICI's attention weights, in a TME context, literally measure which specific neighbors are driving T cell exhaustion. A high-weight edge from a tumor cell to a T cell, with ablation showing PD-1 drops when that tumor cell type is removed, is direct computational evidence of the immunosuppressive mechanism operating in that specific microenvironment.

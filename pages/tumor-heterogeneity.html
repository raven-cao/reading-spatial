Tumor Heterogeneity

A tumor is an ecosystem, not a clone

What heterogeneity means

A tumor starts from a single mutant cell — a clone. But as it grows and cells continue dividing, additional mutations accumulate. A subpopulation with a new mutation becomes a subclone — a nested group within the tumor with its own distinct characteristics. Over time, the tumor contains multiple subclones with different mutation profiles, growth rates, drug sensitivities, and immune evasion strategies.

Darwinian selection within a tumor

Chemo and immune pressure act as selection forces. Subclones that happen to carry resistance mutations survive and expand. Subclones that are sensitive are eliminated. This is evolution in fast-forward — the tumor doesn't "learn" resistance, it selects for it. The pre-existing heterogeneity provides the raw material; selection pressure determines the outcome.

Why a single biopsy is insufficient
Different regions of a tumor may contain different subclones. A biopsy samples one location. If the resistant subclone is spatially segregated from the sample site, a genomic analysis will miss it — and then the tumor "inexplicably" relapses. Spatial approaches begin to address this.

Relevance to spatial transcriptomics

Heterogeneity is partly spatial: different regions of the tumor have different clonal compositions, different immune infiltration, different signaling environments. A single-cell average is inadequate. AMICI's single-cell, spatially-resolved approach is motivated exactly by this — the answer might be different in the tumor core than the margin, in one subclone than another.

Immune System

Immune Checkpoints

The brakes tumors use — and the drugs that remove them

What checkpoints are for

The immune system needs off-switches. Without them, T cells would attack healthy tissue — the mechanism behind autoimmune diseases. PD-1 on T cells binds PD-L1 on normal cells, signaling "don't attack me, I'm healthy." CTLA-4 is another brake controlling early T cell activation in lymph nodes.

How tumors hijack them

Tumor cells upregulate PD-L1, especially under inflammatory conditions (IFN-γ exposure). When T cells approach to attack, PD-L1 binds their PD-1, triggering the self-tolerance signal. The T cell stands down. The tumor has stolen the language of healthy tissue to protect itself.

Checkpoint inhibitors

Pembrolizumab (Keytruda) and nivolumab block PD-1. Ipilimumab blocks CTLA-4. By occupying these receptors, they prevent tumor PD-L1 from sending the "stand down" signal. The brakes are removed; T cells can attack.

Why this works in MSI but not MSS
MSI tumors have many more neoantigens (high mutation burden). When the brakes are removed, there are many targets for T cells to recognize — the immune response is strong. MSS tumors have fewer mutations, fewer neoantigens, and the T cells that were being suppressed may also be more deeply exhausted. Releasing PD-1 alone isn't enough to restart an exhausted, epigenetically locked T cell.

2018 Nobel Prize

James Allison (CTLA-4) and Tasuku Honjo (PD-1) shared the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for discovering these checkpoint mechanisms and enabling their therapeutic use.
